How to Use this toolkit

Purpose & Navigation

This toolkit is designed for Tribal Governments, community members, and allied organizations navigating energy project proposals on or near sovereign lands. It is not intended to tell communities what to decide, but rather provides transparent information and questions needed to make decisions that are truly informed, truly free, and truly yours.

Energy developers approaching Native lands are increasingly pitching hydrogen and waste-to-energy projects as "clean," "green," or "zero-emission" solutions. These claims can be misleading, and the technical complexity of the projects makes them difficult to evaluate without specialized knowledge. This toolkit translates that technical complexity into accessible, actionable information.

How to Navigate

If a developer has already approached your community

Start with Section 06 (The Greenwashing Guide) to identify potential red flag language in their pitch, then check Section 05 (The Companies) to read case studies on companies that have been involved in these projects. Use Section 07 (The Due Diligence Checklist) before any formal engagement or as soon as possible if engagement has already begun.

If you want to understand the technologies

Start with Sections 02 and 03 for plain-language overviews of hydrogen and waste-to-energy technologies, including what each one actually emits and what the potential environmental and health risks are associated with each technology.

If you want to understand your legal and regulatory rights

Go to Section 04 (The Regulations) for an overview of the agencies involved, how regulatory gaps are exploited, and how to ensure Free, Prior, and Informed Consent is respected.

If you want to explore alternatives

See Section 08 (The Alternatives) for an overview of alternative renewable energy options and how to achieve a Just Transition while maintaining Tribal energy sovereignty.

A note on framing: This toolkit uses the term "false solutions" to describe projects that are marketed as clean or green but fail to deliver genuine environmental or community benefit. This framing is commonly used in climate justice and environmental justice circles. However, the toolkit recognizes that not all "green" energy technologies are equivalent, and that technologies which are produced from truly renewable energy sources have legitimate climate potential and as such are treated differently throughout this document.

The Technologies: Hydrogen

Production Methods, Environmental & Health Risks

Hydrogen energy is not a single technology—rather, it can be produced using a spectrum of methods with very different environmental profiles. The color-coding system below (green, blue, grey, pink) is commonly used in the industry, but developers do not always disclose which type they are using, and in some cases may employ one technology as a "bridge" to transition to another. In some cases, developers and regulatory agencies may consider one technology as 'clean' by means of other technologies that offset or repurpose carbon emissions.

It is important to always ask informed guiding questions that help communities understand which technology is actually being developed, what infrastructure is being built, where energy and water resources are coming from, and what the environmental and health impacts and risks are.

GREEN
Production Method

Electrolysis of water using renewable electricity (solar or wind). No fossil fuels involved in production.

Environmental Risks

High water consumption. Land footprint for associated renewable infrastructure. Low carbon when renewable energy is truly clean.

Health Risks

Minimal direct health risks if produced cleanly. Explosion/leak risk at storage and transport facilities requires safety planning.

Most promising — but context matters
BLUE
Production Method

Natural gas reforming (steam methane reforming) combined with carbon capture and storage (CCS) technology.

Environmental Risks

Methane leakage during extraction and transport. CCS is often incomplete — industry rates of 80–90% capture leave significant residual emissions. Fossil fuel dependent.

Health Risks

Air quality impacts from methane and NOx emissions. Communities near gas infrastructure bear cumulative burden from upstream extraction.

Fossil fuel dependent — greenwashing risk
GREY
Production Method

Natural gas reforming with no carbon capture. Currently the most common form of hydrogen production globally.

Environmental Risks

High CO2 emissions — roughly 10 kg CO2 per kg H2. Significant upstream methane leakage. No climate benefit over fossil fuels.

Health Risks

NOx and particulate emissions near production facilities. Upstream extraction impacts on air and water quality in source communities.

Not clean — rarely disclosed upfront
PINK
Production Method

Electrolysis powered by nuclear energy. Produces no direct carbon emissions during hydrogen production.

Environmental Risks

Long-term nuclear waste storage. Thermal discharge into water bodies. Risk of radioactive contamination if incident occurs.

Health Risks

Radiation risk for workers and nearby communities. Long-term waste storage concerns for Tribal lands in proximity to storage sites.

Contested — nuclear trade-offs often hidden

Key Takeaways for Communities

  • Always ask which type of hydrogen is being proposed and what the energy source is. A project simply called "hydrogen" or "clean hydrogen" is not automatically green hydrogen. If possible, seek external third party verification to assess technologies being proposed.
  • Blue hydrogen is dependent on natural gas, which is a fossil fuel. While blue hydrogen is supposed to utilize carbon capture technologies to offset emissions, it is important to question exactly how those emissions will be offset. For example, if carbon emissions are to be "stored" underground (such as in Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)), where are they being stored and what are the environmental impacts of the storage technology? If the carbon captured is to be repurposed and sold (such as in carbon sales to soda beverage companies), what are the environmental impacts of transporting the carbon byproduct to those companies?
  • Green hydrogen has legitimate climate potential but carries significant water use implications—a critical concern for communities with existing water rights conflicts. It is critical to understand where the water used to produce green hydrogen is coming from, and whether this impacts communities already burdened by access to clean and dependable water sources.
  • Many projects claiming federal "clean hydrogen" incentives under the IRA are blue, not green. Ask for detailed documentation of the project's energy source and planned infrastructure before any discussions proceed.

Important note: during the Biden-era administration and their introduction of the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), Section 45V of the IRA specifically designated incentives for the development of hydrogen energy projects, which sparked large-scale financing of hydrogen hubs. After the 2025 Trump administration took office and implemented the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), the timelines for projects depending on those incentives was cut short, resulting in withdrawals of funding for several large-scale projects. For those projects in which financial backing remained, the drastically reduced timeline to claim incentives under the IRA may have resulted in projects being pushed forward under circumstances which were not initially planned for.

The Technologies: Waste-to-Energy

Incineration, Gasification & Pyrolysis

Waste-to-Energy (WTE) projects convert municipal solid waste, biosolids, or other waste streams into energy. Across all cases reviewed for this toolkit, WTE technologies are consistently problematic — no WTE process is truly "zero waste" or emission-free, despite how they are frequently marketed.

A critical red flag: many WTE developers now partner with Regional Hydrogen Hubs to access federal DOE funding and rebrand their projects as "hydrogen" companies. This makes them harder to identify and track. A project branded as "hydrogen" may in fact be a waste gasification facility.

INCINERATION
How It Works

Burns municipal solid waste at high temperatures (800–1000°C) to generate steam that drives turbines and produces electricity.

Environmental Risks

Produces dioxins, furans, heavy metals, and bottom ash requiring landfill disposal. CO2 and NOx emissions comparable to fossil fuel plants. Ash is a hazardous waste.

Health Risks

Linked to increased rates of cancer, respiratory illness, and cardiovascular disease in surrounding communities. Historically sited disproportionately near low-income communities and communities of color.

Most common WTE pitch to Tribal lands
GASIFICATION
How It Works

Partially oxidizes waste at high heat to produce syngas (a mixture of carbon monoxide and hydrogen), which is then combusted for energy.

Environmental Risks

Produces tar, slag, and contaminated wastewater. Syngas combustion releases CO2, NOx, and particulates. Ash contains concentrated heavy metals.

Health Risks

Carcinogen emissions including benzene and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). Worker exposure risk. Community air quality impacts depend heavily on facility management quality.

Often marketed as "cleaner" than incineration
PYROLYSIS
How It Works

Heats waste in the absence of oxygen to produce bio-oil, syngas, and char. Sometimes marketed as "chemical recycling" or "advanced recycling."

Environmental Risks

Produces hazardous liquid and solid byproducts. Emission profiles are difficult to characterize fully. Energy-intensive process may require fossil fuel inputs.

Health Risks

Poorly regulated. Limited long-term health data. PFAS ("forever chemicals") and heavy metals can concentrate in char, which may be marketed as fertilizer — posing serious soil and groundwater contamination risks.

Least regulated — highest greenwashing risk

The "No-Burn" Claim

Several gasification and pyrolysis companies — including H-Cycle and Sierra Energy — market their technologies as "non-combustion" or "no-burn." This is a technicality. While the initial waste processing stage may not involve direct combustion, the syngas or bio-oil produced is subsequently burned to generate power, releasing the same pollutants the "gasification" stage supposedly avoided. The claim is misleading and should be treated as a red flag.

The "Zero Waste" Claim

No WTE process produces zero waste. All three technologies generate toxic solid residues (ash, slag, or char) that require disposal. "Zero waste to landfill" claims typically mean the ash or char is reclassified and sent elsewhere — often to communities that have even less regulatory protection. Always ask: where does the ash go?

The Regulations

How Projects Get Approved — and How Protections Break Down

Understanding the regulatory environment is essential for evaluating any energy project proposal. Tribal sovereign lands exist at a complex intersection of federal, state, and tribal jurisdiction — a complexity that developers actively exploit to minimize oversight and accelerate approvals.

Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC)

FPIC is an internationally recognized right of Indigenous peoples to give or withhold consent to projects affecting their lands and communities. It is enshrined in the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, Articles 19, 26, and 32) and adopted in multiple international frameworks. It requires that consent be:

  • Free — given without coercion, manipulation, or improper pressure
  • Prior — obtained before any project activities begin, not after plans are finalized
  • Informed — based on complete, accurate, and accessible information about the project

In practice, FPIC is frequently violated. Developers often conduct "consultation" only after project design is complete, engage only with council leadership rather than the broader community, and frame outreach as community engagement rather than consent. Receiving a presentation is not the same as giving consent. Legal examples of FPIC violations include: the Dakota Access Pipeline controversy (DAPL was approved without meaningful consultation with the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe before construction began) and the Tonto Natural Bridge State Park uranium mining proposal (community concerns were dismissed despite extensive prior engagement). These cases demonstrate how developers and agencies can proceed without genuine FPIC, making legal vigilance essential.

Federal Agency Roles

EPA
Oversees environmental review under NEPA and enforces Clean Air Act and Clean Water Act standards. Enforcement on Tribal lands is inconsistent — the agency has limited staff and resources dedicated to Indian Country. Can be a point of advocacy for communities seeking independent environmental review.
BIA (Bureau of Indian Affairs)
Manages trust land approvals and right-of-way decisions for energy infrastructure crossing Tribal lands. Chronically underfunded and understaffed, leading to slow reviews and inconsistent protection. Has its own NEPA process for trust land actions.
FERC
Federal Energy Regulatory Commission — relevant for hydrogen pipeline infrastructure and natural gas facilities. Jurisdiction is contested on sovereign lands. FERC has historically prioritized infrastructure development over community concerns.
DOE
Administers the Hydrogen Hub program and IRA clean energy incentives. The DOE has a stated commitment to Tribal consultation but funding timelines create significant pressure on communities to move quickly. DOE approval does not constitute environmental or community approval.
State Agencies
Jurisdiction of state environmental agencies over Tribal sovereign lands is contested and varies by state, treaty, and specific land status. Developers sometimes route projects to minimize state oversight by using trust land or tribal enterprise structures.

Common Developer Bypass Strategies

Categorical Exclusions

Developers seek "categorical exclusions" from full Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) review by fragmenting a larger project into smaller components, each of which individually qualifies for exclusion. The cumulative impact is never fully assessed.

Tribal Enterprise Routing

Some developers structure projects through Tribal enterprise entities to reduce state and federal oversight — exploiting sovereignty protections that were designed to protect communities, not facilitate developer access.

Jurisdictional Ambiguity

When federal, state, and tribal agencies each claim or disclaim jurisdiction, developers operate in the resulting grey zone to minimize review requirements. Communities can push back by demanding clarity on which agency has jurisdiction and what standards apply.

IRA & DOE Funding as a Pressure Mechanism

The Inflation Reduction Act and DOE Hydrogen Hub program have created billions of dollars in clean energy incentives — and significant time pressure. Developers regularly use federal funding timelines as leverage, framing delay as "missing out" on once-in-a-generation funding. Communities have the right to take the time they need. Federal funding opportunities do not expire overnight, and the urgency framing is frequently a sales tactic.

The Greenwashing Guide

Buzzwords, Red Flags & What They Really Mean

Greenwashing refers to the practice of marketing a product or project as environmentally friendly when it is not — or when the environmental claims are misleading, incomplete, or unverifiable. Energy developers routinely use greenwashing language when pitching projects to Tribal communities. This section provides a plain-language guide to common terms and what to watch for.

Buzzword Glossary

High Risk
"Zero Emission" / "Zero Emission Facility"
Typically refers only to direct on-site emissions, excluding upstream production, transportation, or waste disposal. Almost no energy facility is truly zero-emission when the full lifecycle is considered. Ask: zero emissions of what, measured where, and across what scope?
High Risk
"Carbon Neutral" / "Net Zero"
Often achieved through carbon offsets rather than actual emission reductions at the project site. Critical distinction: purchased offsets (e.g., paying another entity to reduce emissions elsewhere) are not the same as actual sequestration (capturing and permanently storing carbon on-site). Offsets are frequently of questionable quality, unverified, and may not represent genuine carbon reduction. Does not mean zero emissions from the facility. Ask: how is carbon neutrality achieved — through actual emission reductions at the site, or through purchased offsets? If offsets are used, are they verified through third-party auditing?
High Risk
"Non-Combustion" / "No-Burn" Technology
Used by gasification and pyrolysis companies to distinguish their process from traditional incineration. Misleading — the syngas or bio-oil produced is subsequently burned to generate energy, releasing pollutants. The "non-combustion" label applies only to the first stage of a multi-stage process. Always ask what happens to the syngas or fuel produced.
High Risk
"Clean Hydrogen" / "Green Hydrogen"
No universal legal definition. "Clean hydrogen" under current IRA guidance includes blue hydrogen (fossil fuel-based with CCS), which is not green hydrogen. Projects may claim clean hydrogen status while using natural gas as a primary input. Ask: what color hydrogen, and what is the energy source for production?
Caution
"Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF)" / "Sustainable Fuels"
A marketing term for fuels derived from waste or biomass. Does not mean zero-emission — SAF still produces CO2 when burned. Often involves incineration-adjacent processing of municipal solid waste or plastic. Ask: what feedstock is used, and what are the full lifecycle emissions?
Caution
"Community Benefit Agreement" / "Revenue Sharing"
Can be meaningful if legally binding and independently negotiated. Frequently used as a goodwill gesture without enforceable obligations. The presence of a CBA does not indicate a project is environmentally sound. Ask: is this legally binding, who negotiated it, and can it be independently reviewed?
Caution
"DOE-Funded" / "Federally Backed" / "Part of a Hydrogen Hub"
Federal funding does not imply environmental approval or community benefit. DOE Hydrogen Hub designations cover multiple hydrogen types and do not require green hydrogen. Federal backing is frequently used to create urgency and pressure fast decisions. Ask: what are the specific conditions of the federal funding, and what community input was required?
Caution
"Thermal Conversion" / "Advanced Conversion Technology"
Umbrella terms used to describe gasification, pyrolysis, or plasma arc processes without specifying what they are. Designed to avoid the negative associations of "incineration." Ask: what specific process is used, what are the inputs, and what are the outputs including waste streams?
Context-Dependent
"Closed-Loop System" / "Circular Economy"
Red flag for PFAS and heavy metal contamination: This term suggests waste is fully recycled with no residual emissions or byproducts. In practice, no energy system is fully closed-loop. Biosolids-to-energy and waste gasification companies frequently use this language to obscure the fact that hazardous chemicals — particularly PFAS ("forever chemicals") — concentrate in the resulting char, slag, or ash residue. PFAS does not break down and can leach into soil and groundwater for decades. Ask: what happens to solid residues (char, ash, slag)? Have they been tested for PFAS and heavy metals? Will the char or residue be marketed as a soil amendment or fertilizer (a known pathway for soil and groundwater contamination)?

The Due Diligence Checklist

10 Questions to Ask Before Engaging with Any Energy Developer

Use this checklist before any formal engagement or agreement with an energy developer. These questions are designed to surface greenwashing, protect sovereignty, and ensure your community has the information needed to make a truly informed decision. You have the right to ask all of these questions — and to receive complete, written answers before proceeding.

About the Technology
1
What type of hydrogen or WTE technology is being proposed — exactly — and what is the energy source? Do not accept generic terms like "clean hydrogen," "thermal conversion," or "advanced energy." Ask for the specific process name, the feedstock (what goes in), and the energy source. If it is hydrogen, ask what color and demand documentation of the electricity source.
2
What are the projected air, water, and soil emissions at the project site — not just at the "system" level? Developers often present lifecycle or "system-level" emissions that exclude on-site impacts. Ask for specific emission estimates at the facility location, including all criteria pollutants (NOx, particulates, VOCs), any toxic air contaminants, and wastewater discharge volumes.
3
Has an independent environmental impact assessment been conducted — one not funded by the developer? Developer-funded environmental studies have significant conflicts of interest. Demand an independent review. If one has not been conducted, this should be a prerequisite before any agreement.
About the Company
4
Has this company operated a similar project before — and what was the outcome? Ask for references from completed projects, not just proposals or pilots. Research the company's track record independently. For H-Cycle, research the Plasco Energy Group connection. For any company, search for news coverage, regulatory violations, and community complaints at prior sites.
5
Does the company have a history of regulatory violations, fines, or community complaints? Search EPA's ECHO database (echo.epa.gov) for violations. Search state environmental agency records. Contact environmental justice organizations in areas where the company has operated.
6
Is this company affiliated with — or a rebranding of — a failed project elsewhere? Research the leadership team's prior affiliations. Companies with failed projects frequently rebrand or restructure. H-Cycle's leadership ties to Plasco Energy Group is a documented example of this pattern.
About the Process
7
Were community members — not just council leadership — meaningfully consulted before the project was designed? FPIC requires that the full community, not just elected leadership, have the opportunity to participate in decisions. Ask how community input was solicited, who was included, and how it shaped the project design. A presentation to council is not the same as community consultation.
8
What federal or state funding is the developer accessing, and what conditions come with it? Federal funding through IRA or DOE Hydrogen Hubs comes with specific requirements — ask for documentation. Funding conditions may constrain future community decision-making or create obligations that are not immediately apparent.
9
What legally binding protections exist if the developer fails to meet commitments? Ask for specific, enforceable mechanisms — not just promises. Community Benefit Agreements should be reviewed by independent legal counsel before signing. Ask what happens if the company goes bankrupt, is sold, or fails to meet emission standards.
About Alternatives
10
Has the community been presented with genuinely renewable alternatives — solar, wind, community-owned — at comparable scale? Any developer proposing a hydrogen or WTE project should be able to compare it to solar and wind alternatives. If they cannot or will not make this comparison, that is a red flag. See Section 08 for an overview of alternative options.

The Alternatives

Centering Energy Development Decisions Around Just Transition and Indigenous Sovereignty

Hydrogen and waste-to-energy projects are not the only path to energy sovereignty and economic development. Communities deserve alternatives that center control, benefit, and environmental protection. This section highlights approaches that have demonstrated success on Tribal lands and provides evaluation frameworks to assess whether any energy project truly serves community needs.

Community-Owned Solar & Renewable Energy

Community-owned solar keeps economic benefits and decision-making power local. Successful examples: The Navajo Nation's NTUA operates utility-scale solar with local job creation, training programs, and profits supporting Tribal infrastructure. Standing Rock's solar cooperative combines youth workforce development with energy sovereignty and water protection priorities.

Evaluating an Energy Project Proposal

Use this checklist to assess whether a project centers community benefit and environmental protection:

✓ Look for these markers:
  • Community (not corporations) makes all major decisions
  • Quality jobs with training and career pathways, not temporary low-wage work
  • Community ownership and profit-sharing in project revenue
  • No compromise on water rights, air quality, sacred sites, or land health
  • Project timeline respects community priorities, not developer urgency
✗ Watch out for these red flags:
  • Corporate control with limited Tribal government authority
  • Temporary or low-wage jobs; skilled work goes to outside workers
  • Profits leave the community or go primarily to external investors
  • Environmental compromise (water, air, sacred sites, or land required)
  • Artificial urgency and "now or never" pressure to decide quickly

The energy transition presents real economic opportunities for Tribal nations—but only if communities retain control and prioritize long-term community wellbeing over short-term corporate profit. Just Transition frameworks ensure that "clean" energy development also means just development: community-controlled, environmentally protective, economically equitable, and aligned with Tribal sovereignty.
